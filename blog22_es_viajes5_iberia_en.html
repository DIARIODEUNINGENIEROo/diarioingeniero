Day 1, Toledo to Merida


I left Toledo in the morning on my way to Mérida, the first large city and the most monumental of the trip together with Cáceres and perhaps one of the cities with the most history in Spain, whose streets hide great treasures from Roman times under their floors. or later. This city was chosen by the Roman elites to retire and spend their last years of life. Merida comes from emerita and means retired.

Mérida is famous for its Roman theater and amphitheatres, but there are many other attractions for this city. I visited it when I was little and I just remember these places. On this visit I didn't spend a single euro on monuments and I took the whole day very different from the visit when I was little in which I would stop for no more than four hours and see four different sites.
Park the car not far from the Roman theater and without paying 1 euro since I left it in a residential area very close to a supermarket. I would start with what is close to the theater, such as the Roman museum where the original figures of both the amphitheater and the theater are. It also keeps many objects found in excavations throughout the autonomous community of Extremadura. Many mosaics of Roman residences are displayed on the walls of this museum which, in addition to being large, is quite tall, three floors and almost 20 meters high. In the following image I show what a Roman house of the time was like, from the archaeological museum. It is the reference Roman museum of the entire Iberian Peninsula, ahead of the Evora museum in Portugal.


It is also divided into Roman times and has a large collection of coins and vessels from the time.


After concluding my visit to the museum, I would visit the obelisk and the basilica of Santa Eulalia, a basilica that is quite impressive on the inside but do not go into the crypt, which requires a fee and houses one of the oldest images of this saint
This basilica was the first Christian temple erected in Hispania after the Peace of Emperor Constantine; This church was therefore built as a martyr's basilica in memory of Eulalia de Mérida; For this reason, during the dawn of the Middle Ages, it became a place of pilgrimages that arrived from Western Europe and North Africa. It is a World Heritage Site largely due to the excavations that were carried out in its subsoil and brought to light the first burial mound of Santa Eulalia from the 4th century. The original paintings are in very good condition.
Later, towards the center, I would visit the Roman forum in which the portico is preserved, the temple of Diana whose square has recently been restored, the arch of Trajan as one of the Major Roman remains.
The so-called Temple of Diana is a Roman temple built in the 1st century AD. C. in the city of Augusta Emerita, capital of the Roman province of Lusitania, current Mérida (Spain). It was built in the municipal forum of the Roman city following the usual configuration of the temples of classical antiquity and is the only Roman religious building that has survived in Mérida in an acceptable state of conservation. It was actually dedicated to the imperial cult, not to the goddess Diana, and must have been one of the main temples in the city, judging by its dedication and the preeminent place it occupied in urban space. Since 1993 it has been declared a World Heritage Site as part of the Mérida Archaeological Ensemble.

Then I visited the fortress and the Visigothic museum that is inside a convent as monuments from other times. The citadel was built by Abderramán II in the year 835 AD. C. as a bastion to control the city, which since the year 805 had continuously rebelled against the Emirate domain. This makes it the oldest surviving Muslim fortification on the Iberian Peninsula. The fortification consists of a square enclosure 130 meters on each side capable of housing a good number of troops.
As religious buildings the co-cathedral of Santa María la Mayor and the church of Carmen and the cathedral square. The co-cathedral is important in that it was built in the 13th century, after the reconquest, by the oldest Catholic cathedral in the peninsula, the Visigothic cathedral of Santa María de Jerusalen. Only a pillar remains of it, which is in one of the chapels. Nearby, I would visit the national inn of Mérida, built on a unique palace.
After seeing the entire center, I would go out to the outskirts through the river park. First of all, there is the iron railway bridge built in the 19th century and more towards the end is where one of the hidden wonders of Mérida is found, the aqueduct of Los Milagros. One of the longest preserved aqueducts in Spain and the most unknown since being on the outskirts few know about it. With this I would go on my way to the hotel north of Mérida, which would allow me to see the province of Cáceres better, since this trip, except for Mérida, was focused on the province of Cáceres.

Day 2 Montanchez to Trujillo


The first point to visit in the morning is the town of Montanchez, whose castle can be visited completely and is located at the top of the town. The town is quite common to other towns in the area, with a fairly old church, El Salvador, and a recently restored main square.

The second town, Alcuescar, is somewhat larger than the first. It has a larger church than the one in Montanchez, that of the Asunción. But what stands out the most is having the Church of Santa Lucía del Trampal just 3 kilometers from the town. One of the few churches in all of Spain still standing from the Visigoth period. It is a mix between Visigothic and Mozarabic and because it was hidden in the undergrowth for centuries, it has been preserved in very good condition. It was discovered by a looter who was looking for remains of the French war of independence and came across this construction. Then, before warning the people, the entire area would be cleaned and the first archaeological works carried out. All the remains of this church are in the archaeological museum of Cáceres. Although they have built a building to accommodate a guard and make an explanation of the building as well as a visual projection.




After seeing these two towns, I would see Valdefuentes, very close to the hotel where I stayed, which is just 30km from Cáceres. This town has a very large square and a couple of churches, as well as a large palace of the nobility of that time, today owned by the town hall.

In the afternoon after eating I went to see Guadalupe, and its monastery that although I had visited it when I was a child I hardly remembered anything. The entrance to the monastery church is free and the entrance to the monastery is 5 euros. It is well worth entering as they show you rooms that are authentic cultural gems, full of paintings and precious stones. And it is that Guadalupe for many years has been a place of pilgrimage to the Virgin and gold and jewels have been donated from America. The cloister is one of the largest in Spain, having a large garden, and this monastery came to house the lives of 400 friars. The town also has quite a homogeneity in its buildings, with a cobblestone floor and streets with a multitude of historical arches.


After seeing the monastery, I would go to Trujillo, one of the prettiest and most historic towns in Cáceres and I would dare to say in all of Spain. Such is the density of monuments per square meter, the majority being palaces and religious buildings that it has been chosen along with Cáceres to shoot the mega-film Game of Thrones there and has been re-elected to shoot numerous scenes for the new series, the prequel to Game of Thrones, a series consisting of 10 seasons, one of the longest.
This town had a fairly large fortress in Arab times but its splendor would come thanks to the Spanish conquerors in America. The most famous conqueror is Francisco Pizarro, who has a large palace in the central square and a statue on horseback that is one of the most photographed in Spain. The square is surrounded by palaces since many of his men were from Trujillo and also the cradle of other conquerors. The Palace of the Conquest the most visited that has a large coat of arms on the corner of the building and that of San Carlos. Outstanding religious buildings are the church of San Martín in the Plaza Mayor, the church of Santiago and the church of Santa María la Mayor. In that area there are several defensive towers and walls that give the city that medieval air. It also has more churches further from the center and convents that increase the monumentality of the city. What is missing is the visits to the interiors of the palaces, and that most of the churches only open during liturgical hours and not for tourism, at least when I was there. I was only 3 hours but this town is well worth a whole day to visit it.

Day 3, Cáceres


The main dish of the trip is the visit to Cáceres, which together with Mérida are World Heritage cities.
The "Monumental City of Cáceres" was declared by the Council of Europe as the Third Monumental Ensemble of Europe in 1968 and a World Heritage Site by UNESCO in 1986.
Cáceres is a walled city full of palaces and beautiful squares and tall towers such as the Bujaco tower that leads into the old town. The list of towers in the city is extensive:

Of which I would start with the Palacio de los Carvajales, current site of the Cáceres Provincial Council where you can visit the patio and gardens. I would continue by the palace of the Golfines de Abajo which has been open to visitors for not many years (the one above is a restaurant). This palace has a good collection of neoclassical rooms. The jewel of the palace is the weapons room of which, when making the last reform, the original coffered ceiling was discovered as well as a number of hidden paintings after the last reform in which everything was painted white. All the noble coats of arms and coats of arms are collected, as well as portraits of the different marquises of the palace.
Another essential palace is the Veletas Palace where the museum of the city of Cáceres is located. This museum also occupies The House of Horses. It is of great historical importance due to the fact that it occupies the site where the old Almohad fortress used to be, of which only the cistern remains, whose floor plan is irregular, approximately fifteen meters long by ten meters wide, sixteen supported horseshoe arches by twelve columns, some of Roman origin, forming the five vaulted naves of which the construction consists.
In it there is a large collection of archeology from the Palaeolithic, as well as the Roman, Visigothic (where those found in the church of Santa Lucía del Trampal seen above), medieval and more modern. It also has a temporary exhibition room, in which there was a large area dedicated to modernist painting when I visited it.
Another of the essential palaces is the Cigüeñas Palace where the Cáceres Weapons Museum is located. The building is from the 15th century and is located in the same square as the Palacio de las Veletas, within the Arab fortification.
I would also see from the outside the palace of the counts of Adanero, that of the commander of Alcuescar and that of Montezuma inside the wall from the outside. Somewhat far from the center in the afternoon I would see the palace of Galarza and that of the Marquis of Camarena, with large coats of arms and larger than several in the center.

Day 4, de Medellin to Toledo


Starting with Almoharín, a town with a square and streets typical of the area.

Then I would go to see Medellin as the main course of the day, the birthplace of the best Spanish conqueror in America, Hernan Cortes, conqueror of the entire Valley of Mexico. This city is of Roman origin and the Roman circus and Roman columns and remains have recently been recovered. The importance of the Roman city comes from the great bridge that crosses the river. After the Romans, the Arabs would come and build a great fortification on top of the town. Apart from the old area of ​​the Alcazar, Medellin has a beautiful square with the statue of Hernan Cortes and two or three important churches. Unlike Trujillo, Hernan Cortes hardly lived in the city, since he would live in Mexico and marry a native, so he did not continue with buildings like those of the other town and there was no lineage of conquerors in the town.

Then, finally, I would visit Villanueva de la Serena, which although it is a modern city, one of the most populated in Extremadura and that after the merger with Don Benito will become the third most populated city, it has quite a history.
It has a fairly large church, a large town hall square and a palace in the center.
