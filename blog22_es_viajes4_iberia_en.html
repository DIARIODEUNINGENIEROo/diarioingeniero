Diariodeuningeniero



9th to 13th September of 2021

Dia 1, Toledo a Coria


I left Toledo in the morning on my way to Plasencia, the first large and monumental city of the trip. Stop in Oropesa to see the castle and the county palace, which I couldn't visit inside due to the town's festivities. I was able to see the church of the Asunción from outside and contemplate its beautiful Plaza Mayor and town hall. I was not entertained more than 1 hour.
Shortly after passing Talavera de la Reina, I deviated to the northwest, in order to visit the monastery of Yuste, which is located in Cuacos de Yuste. This monastery, which has two large cloisters and a beautiful church, is important because it was the place where Carlos V, the Spanish man, along with his son Philip II, the most powerful in the world in his time, died. The monastery is a World Heritage Site, but except for the importance of having spent his last years Carlos V has nothing special. It is quite austere and that is if you can visit the rooms where the king lived, although it is not allowed to take photographs in the rooms or in the church.
Once seen, go to see the town, Cuacos de Yuste, whose porticoed square is very well preserved and the fountain is typical of the towns in this area, the towns of La Vera where its paprika, that of La Vera, is famous. There he would also take a bath in the natural pool called El Bañaero, in the stream that runs through it.
. After Cuacos, I would stop at Garganta de la Olla, another very typical town in La Vera. This town is a fairly well-kept and very touristy town where they sell products from the area. It is full of old houses that have preserved their appearance for years and a very well-kept main church. There we can find some natural pools called Las Pilatillas, in which if you continue into the stream I will find several natural waterfalls, although the water is quite cold.
Once the visit of these two towns was over and having eaten, I headed towards Plasencia.
In Plasencia I would leave the car very close to the wall. It was built at the end of the 12th century for defensive purposes, its main promoter being Alfonso VIII of Castile, who in 1186 founded the city to defend both its southern border with the Andalusian territories and its western border with the kingdom of León. Although in the Renaissance and Baroque periods artistic innovations were carried out in its access gates, the rest of the wall has been preserved almost intact since its origins, still enclosing the access to the intramural enclosure today.
It would enter very close to the Puerta del Sol from the northeast through Calle Rey, which leads directly to the Plaza Mayor. In this street there are numerous palatial mansions such as that of the Tamayo or that of the Argollas and it is as important as the Calle Mayor. The Plaza Mayor is quite large and open and in it the Town Hall building stands out. This has a style between Gothic and Renaissance and in it was the jail in its antiquity. A shield of Philip IV stands out on its side.
Going down the street we find the church of San Esteban and the monastery of La Encarnación, before reaching the cathedral square. It is one of the few cathedrals in Spain that preserve its old part and its new part, since the old part was not restored, but rather it was preserved and expanded.

  • Old Cathedral or Church of Santa María that presents the originality of offering two buildings overlapping architecturally and differentiated in time and style. Current Cathedral Museum of Plasencia, is a building that is a very interesting example of transition from Romanesque to Gothic and is one of the greatest architectural values ​​of the city. Its construction began at the beginning of the 13th century and the last proto-Gothic contributions were made in the 15th century
  • New Cathedral begun in 1498 and continued throughout the 16th century in the Gothic-Renaissance style. The main architects of this century participate in its construction more or less directly. The choir, which follows the line of the 15th century Spanish choir stalls, where the stalls of the Catholic Monarchs are shown, and the Bishop's Chair are of particular interest within the new cathedral building.


Not far from the cathedral is the Puerta de Trujillo that leads to the outskirts of the historic center, hitting the river Jerte, the old defensive limit of the city, which is why the most defensive walls are on the other side of the river, in the North part.
Near the cathedral there are also numerous palaces such as the house of the Doctor or Archdeacon of Trujillo of great proportions, the house of the painter Sorolla or the palace of the Monroy or house of the Torres very close to the church of San Nicolas whose image is at the right.
Further up is the palace of the Marquis of Mirabel and the convent of Santo Domingo, currently transformed into a National Parador. Very close to the parador is the church of San Martín in Romanesque style, but with a more recent interior. Pulling towards the wall we find the Berrozana gate and the Alhondiga. And back to the car we will find the church of El Salvador. I would go out through Juan Carlos I avenue and on the way to the motorway I would go to see the Roman aqueduct.

Day 2 Coria to Castello Branco


I left the hotel on the road directly to Coria, a city I wanted to visit because it has one of the three cathedrals in Extremadura.
Founded before the Romans occupied the Iberian Peninsula, and known as Caura, the Romans gave it its current Latin name, Caurium, and this city was later granted Roman citizenship. Later, in the Visigothic era, the Diocese of Coria was created which, except for the years of Muslim occupation, kept Coria as the episcopal seat until the 20th century, when the head of the diocese was split to share it with Cáceres. After the Reconquest, Coria became the capital of a manor to which towns that still bear the name of Coria, such as Guijo de Coria or Casillas de Coria, belonged. After its dissolution, Coria became the capital of the judicial district of Coria.
Currently, Coria is the most important city in the northwest of the province and an important commercial and tourist center, as it conserves a large number of monuments and annually celebrates a festival of tourist interest in Extremadura and nationally in honor of Saint John.

The cathedral seemed quite austere to me, not comparable to that of Plasencia but it is true that it had everything necessary to be a cathedral and not a co-cathedral. Then go through the old jail of the city, today a museum of the city and of the bulls. I was pleasantly surprised to see how carefully they had assembled everything in the museum, including, among others, 5 complete stuffed bulls. Not even in Seville and Huelva did a museum have so many bulls of this nature. In addition, a lot of photographic material since Coria is one of the towns in Spain whose tradition is closely linked to the bull. Remember that in this area the large landowners and ranchers have been the ones who have had the most power in history. Also visit the town hall square and the castle that can be seen in the image together with the wall and its old medieval door on the outside. It is also worth noting the palace of the Dukes of Alba, the Alhondiga and the remains of the old Via Dalmacia in Rome.


I would stop at Mercadona to grab supplies to eat on the go. My next destination was Alcantara, a city known for its Roman bridge. The river that it crosses had a very greenish color from the amount of algae that was there at that time of year, early September after a very hot summer. I was also struck by the dam that is very close to the bridge. After seeing the bridge that must have been heavily defended in the times of Carlos V, who was the one who restored it, since it is the gate from Portugal to all of Extremadura.
In the town, as relevant places, there is the church of Santa María de Almovocar, the Plaza de la Corredera with several noble houses and one in ruins that must have been important in its time due to its size, and the convent of San Benito, which is full of shields. of the Spanish Austrias in its exterior and in its interior. Let us remember that the Alcantaras were families very close to the monarchy and helped a lot in their day to the reconquest of Spain with the Arabs.

After passing this town it would enter Portugal. Due to covid restrictions I had to bring a COVID passport to the hotel. Finally I would arrive at Castelo Branco where I would stay. This city has little heritage, it is more of a residential city that brings together much of the rural population of the area.
As places that can be visited, we find the Renaissance town hall and its square, the market square, the highest castle and its walls, the Palace of the Viscounts of Portalegre, a Renaissance building that houses the seat of the Civil Government of the Castelo Branco district. Let us remember that Portugal is divided into districts, and these in turn could be grouped into regions (which in Spain would be provinces and autonomous communities). Being a total of 18.
As religious buildings, the church of San Miguel, today converted into a co-cathedral, the episcopal palace, the Bishop's house and its gardens, which have several bronze figures and a large pond in the adjacent park. Nearby is the convent of La Gracia and also has a chapel or hermitage such as Nuestra Señora de la Piedad.

Day 3, Castelo Branco and Nisa


I chose this day to visit the Castelo Branco beach pool, which has been a claim since its inauguration 2 years ago. It also coincided with the last day of opening since we were already on September 13, almost the end of summer and also the coronavirus was beginning to rise again.
As there in Portugal they have breakfast very early, from 6:30 to 8:30, I went to find a nice town nearby and stopped in Nisa. This town has a small, fairly well-kept historic quarter, with very homogeneous white houses and a couple of loom museums, one of the typical tasks in the area. On the way to Nisa I would see the river in Porto Rodao, and its exceptional landscape where apparently there are nautical activities such as kayaking on a small pier in the town.
The water of the Praia de Castello Branco was quite good, and also the sun hits almost the entire surface although it is a little cloudy. In addition there were very few people so you did not have to worry much about the safety of your things or anything in the pool. The pool cover is made of a fairly good material, it is not polyfiber, and it also has numerous bridges inside that give it an air more of a beach than a pool, as well as the decoration.
At night I would go to dinner at one of the bars in the area, being dinner time there around 8:00 p.m. and then go up to the highest point of the city, to the castle to be able to contemplate the city at night, being several of emblematic buildings with external lighting.

Day 4, Castelo Branco to Hervas


To make the return to Spain I decided to go through the east of Portugal and Salamanca. The cities I passed were Covilha, the second largest city in the Castelo Branco district, which is a university city in the mountains. From the road you can already see many tall buildings on the mountain and in the distance the historic center and that city originally had a defensive character. Its churches stand out with the typical Portuguese tiles and some escalators that take you up as well as elevators that have also been placed on the other side of the city to have a better panoramic view.



Passing Covilha, he would already be in the district of Guarda and would go to its capital. Guarda is a city with a fairly well-cared-for medieval-style historic center. The cathedral and its great square stand out. It is Gothic and Manueline, the Portuguese style that many of the 15th century buildings in the country have. It has several churches and a tower (Ferreiro) that has been fitted with a modern elevator and good views of the entire town. In the background you can see what remains of the old castle, the homage tower.





From Guarda I went to Spain with destination Ciudad Rodrigo, a very monumental city of Salamanca that does not usually catch anywhere on the way except, as in this case, that you go to Portugal from Salamanca or vice versa. Good time to visit. This city surprised me very positively because the historic center is well delimited by the wall that is completely preserved. This can be explored and has several cannons possibly from the time of the Napoleonic invasion.
It has a somewhat austere Gothic-style cathedral on the outside, except for the portals. One of them has a frieze of great value with characters from the Old Testament carved in stone, as well as details on the capitals and on the door with figures of the evangelists and of Christ. The interior is of a Gothic-style cathedral from that time whose main claim is the Portico de la Gloria or Pordón with representation of the apostles and many figures related to the life of Jesus. The altarpiece of the cathedral was sold to an American museum due to economic needs, being of great value and right now paintings are displayed in its place.
The city has numerous palaces and stately homes such as the Águila Palace, which has a patio with numerous flamboyant-style details, the Palace of the Counts of Yeltes, the Montezuma Palace, the House of Las Cadenas, the Cueto House, the Plaza mayor has a very nice town hall that was a palace in its day and nearby is the Cueto house, the Vazquez house, the Herrera Maldonado house, etc.

As important churches we have the Cerralbo church, very close to the cathedral, and smaller churches as well as a convent whose patio can be seen.
The biggest claim next to the cathedral is the visit to the castle, today a national parador, from which we can see several rooms and climb the tower to see the views. You can also walk through its gardens.

At the end, I would change the destination road to the province of Cáceres, visiting some of the most beautiful towns in Salamanca.



One of them is La Alberca, a town that preserves all its old architecture completely with its narrow streets, its arcades and roofs as if they were from the first day. This town has received several awards and overflows with visitors every day of the year. Many merchants from surrounding towns have a store there, where each portal and house have their stand and where there are several haute cuisine restaurants. His specialty is Iberian ham. There I ate a good Iberian ham sandwich for 3 euros well into the afternoon, and I remember seeing the cutter already tired who hadn't stopped cutting and selling all day. When I arrived there were a lot of people leaving and I was lucky to park in the free car park in town. Later, as there were fewer people left, I was able to visit the town quietly, however in the main square there would be about 50-100 people, and there could be perfectly 500 at other times of the day. It must be a bit overwhelming to come to this town in high season and on weekends with so many people and so little space since the town is quite small.
Another of the towns I passed by was Mogarraz, a town that has a curious tradition in recent years and that is that all the houses have a picture with a portrait of who or who lived in those houses, it is because This is known as The Town of a Thousand Faces. The painter who is from the town would start by making portraits of relatives and close people and in the end he had so much fame that he was doing them to all the inhabitants.
The church already has paintings that reach the top of the tower. The architecture of the houses is well preserved and in addition to a hotel it has several restaurants like Mogarraz.

To sleep, I would stop in a small town where the rural house La Casa de la Abuelita is located, where the owner due to the coronavirus and that there were no visitors that day would leave me the keys to the house. It has a 24-hour service station where I could buy dinner and breakfast.

Dia 5, Hervas a Toledo, through the valley of Jerte


In the morning I left for Hervas, one of the beautiful towns in Spain. It has a well-kept historic center with one of the largest Jewish quarters in a town in Spain. This entire area was home to a large Jewish population that remained on the peninsula despite the expulsion by the Catholic kings. It also has a very old medieval bridge from the Visigoth period and even earlier.
Then I would go to Bejar which is a little further north, a much larger and more important town. The main church of the town stands out, Santa María la Mayor (image), the ducal palace (in the second image) which is today a secondary school, the convent of San Francisco converted into a museum and the extensive wall. This wall can be walked completely and you can see the mountain that surrounds the city, since it is located on a large mountain defensively on which the wall stands.
There is also a statue dedicated to the man with the moss and it is a tradition at the Bejar festivities to dress up in that way and stroll through the streets.

Having seen Bejar, I could either go to Toledo retracing my steps or go up the road to Ávila and then go down again to Cáceres and see the towns of the Jerte valley, visiting the same town of Jerte. For that I had to cross through the port of Tornavacas, in which the road is two-way and in which there are many curves to first reach Jerte, a town located around this road that has the typical architecture of this area with its corresponding main square and church. Further down I would visit Cabezuela del Valle, whose houses and wooden balconies also make the town keep its charm.

Afterwards, it would climb an entire mountain pass until reaching the highest town in all of Extremadura, Piornal, which is at 1200m altitude. The town has a church and a fountain that are very typical of the area, as well as a fountain in Plaza Eras that is adorned with lions on its floor and is quite old. This town has one of the most unique festivals in Extremadura, called the Jarrasplas, a figure that wears a hat with horns and a multitude of colors that has been in procession through the town since ancient times once a year and in which the neighbors have than throw turnips and other vegetables at them as punishment for having stolen cattle or vegetables from the gardens. More and more people come from further afield and that is why it is provided with a good armor in recent times made of fiberglass or carbon. There is even a museum with the oldest and most curious Jarrasplas costumes.

The next town would be Pasarón de la Vera, a town with a recently renovated church with a very colorful and high value altarpiece. It also has ancient polychrome paintings, to the right of the altarpiece. It also highlights the palace of the counts of Osorno.

Finally, it would arrive at Jarandilla de la Vera , a town remembered for being one of the towns that welcomed Carlos V before arriving at the monastery of Yuste for his retirement. While the monastery was being built he was living there, where one of his sons would also live, his son Jeromín, his bastard. The palace where he lived, which has the emperor's coats of arms, and other heraldic shields, is today a national hostel and its patio can be visited.
In the historic center we have a beautiful square that we see in the image and the church of the Virgen de la Soledad.
