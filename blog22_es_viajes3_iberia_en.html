Diariodeuningeniero



August 28th to 31th, 2021

Day 1, Guadalajara to Jaen


I leave my town, Albares de la Ribera and head to Bembibre, where I visit the Ecce Homo sanctuary. This church is more than 5 centuries old and previously there was another temple there, making it one of the oldest churches in all of Bierzo. I had never visited, having been to Bembibre more than 200 times, because it was long before the current city.
Bembibre is the capital of Bierzo Alto and has no more than 10,000 inhabitants. However, it has almost all services and Ponferrada is a stone's throw away, just 13 kms. Due to the large number of large towns around, summers are filled with people, especially on weekend afternoons and nights, with a large number of bars and nightlife areas.

Dia 2, Ubeda, Baeza a Granada


The first thing I went to visit in Baeza was the cathedral square and the Jabalquinto palace, a representative work of the flamboyant style of the fifteenth century, whose most famous palace is that of the infantado de Guadalajara, my city. I would see another of the palaces of this style 2 months later on my visit to Ciudad Rodrigo, the house of the Eagles.
The Baeza Cathedral is quite complete and is from the 15th century in the Gothic style with some Baroque elements inside, and with quite enriched chapels. You can climb the towers and contemplate the incredible views of the city with the Sierra de Cazorla in the background, the landscape being incredible. It also gives us an idea of ​​what the domain of the city was like in times when there was no aviation or long-range weapons. Baeza was an important city in the reconquest in which it was taken by the Christians around 1100 but would be reconquered by the Arabs before the final assault and would give the gate to Granada.
Once the entire Iberian Peninsula was taken and conquered, the walls would be pulled down by order of Isabel La Católica for the growth of the city. However, there is still a small piece left and today it is the siege museum that I saw the day before.
Apart from the cathedral, the Jabalquinto palace, the university palace (where Antonio Machado studied) that is annexed to this palace, we have to see the Salcedo palace, and other palaces and a rich religious heritage with a multitude of churches, yes well they are not very old except for the church of the holy cross which is quite small and has very old polychrome paintings.
The most important churches are the church of El Salvador, the church of San Francisco and its ruins since it is one of the old ones and was a monastery of which only the church remains today. The convent of San Francisco that took its name can also be visited and the church of San Ignacio.

In Úbeda, I first passed by the Santiago hospital, which I only saw from the outside. However, it is a real gem inside, with rooms with frescoes by the best painters of the time and a large patio. inside. I moved from the southwest of the city to the east, seeing the church of San Isidoro, the church of Santo Domingo and arriving at one of the most important squares as the nerve center of Úbeda, the Plaza de Andalucía with the convent and its church of the Holy Trinity. Nearby is the church of San Nicolas, one of the oldest in Romanesque and Gothic style. Looking east, you would see the convent of the Carmelite Barefoot near the food market, the palace of Luis Cueva, the archaeological museum of Úbeda, which is free, highly recommended for being an old Arab palace that preserves exposed Mozarabic polychrome coffered ceilings.
Almost at noon I would shoot to see the most important of the city to the south, the church of San Pablo, the Plaza de Mayo, and the sacred Capilla Mayor de Úbeda, which has the status almost of a cathedral and that can get a ticket set with the cathedral of Baeza for discount. It has a magnificent golden altarpiece and in the center is buried a noble family related to kings of the time. Next to it is the Parador de Úbeda, the Palacio de las Cadenas and the town hall. In front of the palace is the basilica of Santa María de los Reales Alcázares, which together with the Sacra chapel and the church of San Pablo form the most important thing to see.
Later, I would go to eat very close to the Sacra Capilla and I would see the walls of that part, I would continue to see numerous churches, the church of Santo Tomas, San Millan, San Lorenzo and palaces such as the palace of the counts of Guadiana, that of Ordoñez Sandoval, that of Orozco, that of Anguis Medinilla, the Torres house, etc.
Úbeda undoubtedly has more palaces than Baeza and had much more inhabitants, but Baeza was one of the favorites of the Catholic Monarchs and Carlos V, so more important works began to be done, proof of this is the cathedral. Without a doubt, they deserve to be recognized as a world heritage site.

It would then head towards Granada and make a stop at Martos, a town that originally had a defensive role because it was born on the same mountain. The town can already be seen from the road where the white houses and apartments overlook the castle on top and the great churches of its historic center. So I would go up to the top of the town, which cost me a lot, and I would reach the homage tower of the old citadel. There I entered the church of Santa Marta, first together with the town hall, which has a very ornate Baroque doorway, and then in that of Santa María, which is very large. It would also pass through the hermitage of Santa Lucía.
After Martos stop at Alcala La Real. This town is one of the most important in the province of Jaén due to its long history and the fortification it had. First, I visited the Iglesia de la Consolación in the center and the fountain with 3 spouts next to it. Then I went to the town hall square and headed for Granada. I have yet to see the most important part of the city, the fortification of La Mota and its main church, which was once a mosque. There was also a monastery and the only thing that would be saved would be the church. It's all about the old citadel whose towers are very well preserved. Of importance is also the monastery of the Incarnation, the Capuchin convent and the church of San Antonio Rojas.

Day 3, Granada


First I leave the hostel which is in the heart of Granada, next to the Paseo de la Bomba next to the Genil River. Two fountains stand out, the fountain of the lions and the fountain of the Granadas.
Continue to the magnificent Basilica de las Angustias where the patron saint of Granada is. Beautiful baroque temple full of details. I continue and arrive at the heart of Granada in one of the two most important squares, El Campillo. With the source of the Battles and the theater Isabel La Católica. I continue towards Avenida de los Reyes Católicos and visit Plaza del Carmen where the town hall is. A little further away we find the golden double, with its Arab patio, a free monument that is among the outstanding monuments that are being promoted in the city and for the others you have to pay a ticket that gives you access to another 5 more. We continue further forward and come across Plaza Isabel La Católica, the second most important plaza next to Campillo.
I continue straight ahead and I arrive at the Royal Palace of the Chancery. Next to it, the Pilar del Toro fountain and the church of San Gil next to the Tourist Office. There I will get information to make a map of what to see after the Alhambra, a must-see in the city. From there I set course for the Alhambra. The first thing I see in the Alhambra as I go up the cobbled slope is the Puerta de la Justicia, and also the Pilar de Carlos V. In order to access the site, it is necessary to buy the ticket that is purchased from the access pavilion at the end. Once inside, I saw everything in this order: the Siete Soils gate, the Abencerrajes palace, the Carros gate, the Santa María church and the Carlos V palace. Inside this palace there is a permanent exhibition hall. The palace is quite impressive from the outside and the courtyard itself.
Once seen, I go to the Puerta de Vino, on the way to the Alcazaba, with steps such as the Plaza de los Aljibes, the Torre Quebrada, the Torre del Cubo, the Torre de la Vela, and heading towards the Jardín de los Adarves. The appointment for the Nasrid palaces was at 2:30 p.m., and after a short walk through the enclosure I will enter.
Once inside the Nasrid palaces, I find in this order the Patio de los Comares, the Comares baths, the Mocárabes room, then the Patio de los Leones, the Dos Hermanas room, the Abencerrajes room, and the room of the Kings. All this is the jewel in the crown of Granada, the most important rooms and architecture that the Arabs left us on the peninsula.
Then I go through the room of Carlos V, the Peinador de la Reina, the Salón de Embajadores, the Torre de Comares, the Patio de la Rejaa, all this later with Carlos V who increased the rooms but conserving all the Arab representations. Once outside the palace I find another palace such as the Partal and the Torre de las Damas, as well as the remains of the Yusuf palace.

Then I walked the entire wall seeing the different towers and gates of the wall until I reached the Generalife, its palace and gardens, as well as the water staircase and the romantic viewpoint. Once I had seen the Generalife, I left the premises arriving at the Puerta de las Granadas.
At the exit, I continued parallel to the Darro river, where I would visit the Arab baths in El Bañuelo, somewhat smaller than those of Granada but completely preserved. We continue with the Puerta del Tablero, the house of Castril, the church of San Pedro and San Pablo, the convent of San Bernardo. I arrive at Paseo de los Tristes where I will eat in a restaurant. There is the Arab house of the Horno de Oro that together with the Dobla de Oro, the Bañuelo, the house of Chapiz that I will see later and the palace of Dar Al Horra are part of the set of those 5 visitable monuments that they are promoting.
From the Albaicín neighborhood I visited the church of San Nicolas and its magnificent viewpoint towards the Alhambra, the great mosque of Granada that also had a viewpoint with good views. Continuing down the square of Cristo de las Azucenas to see the convent of Santa Isabel la Real, which has a very ornate church. Next to this convent we find the Plaza del Gallo with the church of San Miguel, and next to it the palace mentioned before, which is from the same period as the Alhambra. We continue descending to arrive at night at the Basilica of San Juan de Dios and the Royal Monastery of San Jerónimo. To finish, a visit to the cathedral district, with the main chapel where the Catholic kings are buried, the Alcaicería, an old Arab market or bazaar, and the Madraza palace, impressive inside with Arabized decorations and walls like those seen in the Alhambra.
We return along Pavaneras street, perpendicular to the street of the Catholic kings in the direction of the hostel. On the way I saw the convent of San Francisco from the outside, the palace of the Marquises of Casablanca, the building of the General Captaincy, the palace of the Counts of Gabia, the church of the Comendadoras de Santiago and the church of San Matías.

With this we put an end to the visit to Granada where all these monuments will be seen quickly from the outside that will have to be visited inside on another visit.

Day 4, Granada to Motril and to Guadalajara


First, I visited some places that I had yet to see, such as the Royal Room of Santo Domingo, an Arab palace before the Alhambra, and the church of Santo Domingo and San Mateo, next to the palace. Only one room remains of the palace, with its doors and coffered ceilings, as well as remains of excavations of what it was in its day. It complements an exhibition of images and models of the Alhambra to see their resemblance. Then I took the car on the way to Motril.
In Motril I went to the beach passing by the sanctuary of Our Lady of the Head, a church built in the 17th century on the old Arab citadel and located in the Parque de los Pueblos de América. This park is in stark contrast to the arid terrain around it.
Finally, back to Guadalajara, almost 700 kms...
